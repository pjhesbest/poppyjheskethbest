fasTBSEQ: Long vs Short Reads for TB Surveillance

June 2026

One of the projects I am currently involved in is fasTBSEQ, an initiative focused on advancing genomic surveillance of Mycobacterium tuberculosis in Catalonia. This work builds on the ongoing TBSEQCAT programme, which operates in close collaboration with public health teams across the region to integrate sequencing into routine epidemiological surveillance.

TBSEQCAT has already demonstrated the impact of genomic data in understanding transmission dynamics. For example, recent work identified and characterised outbreak clusters in school settings (Prieto-García et al., 2026), alongside several other ongoing investigations. These studies highlight the practical value of sequencing in informing real-time public health responses.

The fasTBSEQ project, funded by the La Marató de TV3 Respiratory Disease call, aims to expand this capability further. The project focuses on strengthening both sequencing capacity and downstream analysis, enabling faster and more comprehensive responses to tuberculosis transmission.

A key innovation within fasTBSEQ is the integration of long-read sequencing technologies, particularly nanopore sequencing, alongside established short-read approaches. The project also explores direct sequencing from sputum samples using metagenomic approaches, reducing the time between sample collection and actionable insight.

Short-read sequencing has long been the foundation of genomic epidemiology, offering high accuracy and scalability. However, long-read sequencing provides important complementary advantages — including improved resolution of complex genomic regions, detection of structural variation, and the ability to generate more complete genome assemblies (Schiffer et al., 2025).
