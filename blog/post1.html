fasTBSEQ: Long vs Short Reads for TB Surveillance

June 2026

One projects I am currently involved in is fasTBSEQ, a new initiative aimed at improving genomic surveillance of Mycobacterium tuberculosis in Catalonia. This work builds on the ongoing TBSEQCAT project, which has been working closely with Public Health in Catalonia to integrate sequencing into routine epidemiological surveillance.

TBSEQCAT has already demonstrated the power of genomic data to identify and track transmission. For example, recent work has helped understand outbreak clusters in school settings ( Prieto-García et al., 2026), alongside several other ongoing investigations. These efforts highlight how sequencing can provide actionable insight in real-world public health contexts.

The fasTBSEQ project, funded by the La Marató de TV3 Respiratory Disease call, aims to take this work further. The goal is to strengthen our capacity to both generate, analyse and incorporate long-read genomic data, enabling faster and more robust responses to tuberculosis transmission by combining whole-genome sequencing Nanopore sequencing technology, in addition to direct sputum (a mixture of mucus and saliva coughed up from the lower respiratory tract) sampled using a metagenomics approach.

A central focus of the project is the comparison between short-read and long-read sequencing technologies. Short-read approaches have been the backbone of genomic epidemiology for years, offering high accuracy and scalability. However, long-read technologies are increasingly attractive due to their ability to resolve complex genomic regions and provide more complete assemblies ( Shiffer et al., 2025).

Understanding how these technologies perform in a real public health setting is critical. By directly comparing them within the Catalonian surveillance framework, we aim to determine where long reads can offer meaningful advantages, and how they can be integrated into existing pipelines.

Ultimately, fasTBSEQ is about improving our ability to detect transmission, characterise outbreaks, and support public health decision-making. It is an exciting step forward for TB genomics in Catalonia, and I am looking forward to sharing more results as the project progresses.
